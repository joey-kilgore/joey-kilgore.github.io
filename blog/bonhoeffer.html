When David and I met a few months back for lunch, I mentioned I had read through Bonhoeffer (a 600-page tome), and he asked if I would be interested in talking about Bonhoeffer for our series on the Saints. To be completely honest, I felt a bit intimidated, but David reminded me I just read 600 pages on him, and I only have 30 minutes to talk anyways! Still a bit intimidated, I proceeded to buy as many books as I could from Amazon written by Bonhoeffer. Now a few months later, here we are, and I couldn't be more excited to talk about one of the many examples of people who have lived out what it means to truly follow Christ.

Bonhoeffer and I actually have a lot in common. His father was also an academic. He grew up with a large family, though his family of eight siblings certainly tops my five. He too was a pianist, and I imagine he would have enjoyed Keith Green as well, and while I'm still working on my PhD, Bonhoeffer received his Doctor of Theology degree at the age of 21 from the University in Berlin in 1927 with his thesis titled 'Sanctorum Communio'.

He had a wonderfully educated and thoughtful family. Along with a grandmother, who later at the age of 91 was told by nazi guards she could no longer shop at her normal department store because it was run by Jews promptly told the guards "She would shop where she liked and did so...ignoring the silly kick line of SA men stationed in front."" [p. 156-157]

While this family had a wide array of talents, pietist religion was certainly not one of them. Rather than regularly attending church, typically only going for more 'important' services, they had a home regularly filled with scripture. Even as their father may have not described himself as Christian [p. 14], his wife's faith "was most evident in the values that she and her husband taught their children" [p. 12]. Sabine, Bonhoeffer's beloved twin sister, remembered "in all that pertained to our education, our parents stood united as a wall. There was no question of one saying one thing and the other saying something else" [p. 14]. Furthermore, Sabine mentions "[her father possessed] great tolerance that left no room for narrow-mindedness and broadened the horizons of our home. He took it for granted that he would try to do what was right and expected much from us" [p.15]. "Karl Bonhoeffer taught his children to speak only when they had something to say. He did not tolerate sloppiness of expression any more than he tolerated self-pity or boastful pride" [p. 15]. One could say that this house had no place for a 'cheap grace', a phrase that Dietrich would later expand into his early and great work The Cost of Discipleship.

With the groundwork laid, and we can thank Paula and Karl Bonhoeffer for that, we can begin to see what the upbringing of this German boy in the most tumultuous time in recent history produces.

If we are to have one takeaway from this, it was that Bonhoeffer had a complete faith in knowing "for by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast. We are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand, that we should walk in them" [Eph 2:8-10]. Even further, he knew that God had an individual plan for each person, and God's plan for Bonhoeffer was filled with twists, turns, subversion, and imprisonment. But to understand more about what Bonhoeffer stood for, we must know what he stood against.

Within one month of Adolf Hittler being chosen as Chancellor, the Reichstag, which is the German parliamentary building, was set ablaze, and Hitler was able to push an emergency decree:
"Restrictions on personal liberty, on the right of free expression of opinion, including freedom of the press; on the rights of assembly and association; and violations of the privacy of postal, telegraphic and telephonic communications; and warrants for house searches, orders for confiscations as well as restrictions on property, are also permissible beyond the legal limits otherwise prescribed." [p.149] Beginning his reign of being above the law.

While most of us probably know a lot about this failed Austrian artist turned dictator, I found it interesting to learn about Hitler's relationship, or should I say want to control, over the Church in Germany. Hitler had a deep understanding of public perception and manipulating his popularity. This led him to the realization that if he wants to control Germany, he needs to control the Church. Eventually, Hitler would put into law what would be referred to as the 'Aryan Paragraph' which among many things banned ethnic Jews from pastoring in the German Church, even if they were already ordained.

In response, Bonhoeffer wrote a pamphlet on the Jewish Question, he wrote, "The fact, unique in history, that the Jew is subjected to special laws by the state, solely on the basis of his race and regardless of the religion to which he adheres, presents theologians with two new problems, which must be dealt with separately. How does the church judge this action by the state, and what is the church called upon to do about it? What are the consequences for the church's position toward the baptized Jews in its congregations? Both these questions can only be answered on the basis of a right concept of the church" (The Church and The Jewish Question, 1933).

Bonhoeffer lists 3 options of the church regarding the state [p. 153-4]

1.       Be complicit in state actions under the pretext that the state is ordained by God.
Romans 13 "'1Let every person be subject to the governing authorities. For there is no authority except from God, and those that exist have been instituted by God. 2Therefore whoever resists the authorities resists what God has appointed, and those who resist will incur judgment. 3For rulers are not a terror to good conduct, but to bad. Would you have no fear of the one who is in authority? Then do what is good, and you will receive his approval, 4for he is God's servant for your good. But if you do wrong, be afraid, for he does not bear the sword in vain. For he is the servant of God, an avenger who carries out God's wrath on the wrongdoer. 5Therefore one must be in subjection, not only to avoid God's wrath but also for the sake of conscience."

2.       Aid those who are affected by the state's actions. Which we see clearest in Jesus' description of the final judgement.
Matthew 25 "37Then the righteous will answer him, saying, 'Lord, when did we see you hungry and feed you, or thirsty and give you drink? 38And when did we see you a stranger and welcome you, or naked and clothe you? 39And when did we see you sick or in prison and visit you?' 40And the King will answer them, 'Truly, I say to you, as you did it to one of the least of these my brothers, you did it to me.'"

3.       The Church must break the wheel of the state.

1 John 3 - 4Everyone who makes a practice of sinning also practices lawlessness; sin is lawlessness. 5You know that he appeared in order to take away sins, and in him there is no sin. 6No one who abides in him keeps on sinning; no one who keeps on sinning has either seen him or known him. 7Little children, let no one deceive you. Whoever practices righteousness is righteous, as he is righteous. 8Whoever makes a practice of sinning is of the devil, for the devil has been sinning from the beginning. The reason the Son of God appeared was to destroy the works of the devil."

And under the tight grip Hitler had on the German people, Jews were no longer a part of the German Church. But what is the Church if not all peoples in one body?
Galatians 3 - 26for in Christ Jesus you are all sons of God, through faith. 27For as many of you as were baptized into Christ have put on Christ. 28There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is no male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus. 29And if you are Christ's, then you are Abraham's offspring, heirs according to promise."

Many were complicit, option one, especially at the beginning when they had no idea of the horrors to come, and even when they began to know they remained complicit out of fear or corrupted faith in the Reichkirche (Germany Nazi Third Reich Church). This was explored on the front lines of the Nazi Third Reich by Christopher Browning in Ordinary Men "Along with ideological indoctrination, a vital factor touched upon but not fully explored in Milgram's experiments was conformity to the group. The battalion had orders to kill Jews, but each individual did not. Yet 80 to 90 percent of the men proceeded to kill, though almost all of them, at least initially, were horrified and disgusted by what they were doing. To break ranks and step out, to adopt overtly nonconformist behavior, was simply beyond most of the men. It was easier for them to shoot" (Ordinary Men: Reserve Police Battalion 101 and the Final Solution in Poland)

Fewer, though many stories exist, were those who helped those afflicted (option two). We have movies for them, like Schindler's list, and we have heard of the many efforts to help Jews escape the persecution of Nazi Germany. Even fewer were those who were ready to stand-up and try to break the Third Reich itself (option three). Of those three options, be complicit, assist the afflicted, and break the wheel, Bonhoeffer would work towards the ends of options 2 and 3 (assisting, and breaking), because for Bonhoeffer, "silence in the face of evil, is in itself evil: God will not hold us guiltless. Not to speak is to speak. Not to act is to act."

From the beginning, Bonhoeffer saw the separation of Jews from the church as incompatible with the Gospel. It wouldn't be long before Hitler would ban ethnic Jews from serving as clergy, throwing out the Old Testament, working on a translation of the New Testament that removes any 'Jewishness' from it, and replacing the crosses with swastikas. And from this Reinhard Heydrich said "You'll see the day, ten years from now, when Adolf Hitler will occupy precisely the same position in Germany that Jesus Christ has now" [p. 165]. And Hitler to say "It's been our misfortune to have the wrong religion. Why didn't we have the religion of the Japanese, who regard sacrifice for the Fatherland as the highest good? The Mohammedan religion too would have been much more compatible to us than Christianity. Why did it have to be Christianity with its meekness and flabbiness?" [p. 165]

While Bonhoeffer was always openly critical of Hitler and the Nazi regime, he had a powerful understanding of making sure he took the right steps in making his voice heard. In correspondence with his grandmother, he noted that the sooner the church can separate itself from this the better [p. 185]. Bonhoeffer had the restraint to make sure that the defining schism was the German Nazi Church breaking off from the true Faith. He went out and met with various church leaders, many of whom were reluctant to take such a step towards separation in the church, but Bonhoeffer helped lead the charge and pen the words that would start the Confessing Church.

This seems like a point we should take an aside to mention some of the other true believers who joined with Bonhoeffer to stand courageously against the Third Reich. Martin Neimoller, a fellow pastor who helped form the Confessing Church, initially supported Hitler's rise to power, but later became the man who penned the famous words

First they came for the socialists, but I did not speak out
Because I was not a socialist.
Then they came for the trade unionists, but I did not speak out
Because I was not a trade unionist.
Then they came for the Jews, but I did not speak out
Because I was not a Jew.

Then they came for me,
and there was no one left to speak for me.

This is also the time Bonhoeffer is growing his ecumenical contacts, and particularly those with Bishop Bell in London, who would later be an intermediary between the German resistance against Hitler and the British government.

During this time, Bonhoeffer would continue his writings, continue pastoring various communities around Europe, and eventually find himself at Finkenwalde. This was a secret place for training Confessing Church Pastors. These trainees were only slightly younger than Bonhoeffer himself, but they would fill themselves with studying the Word and seeking to understand the Will of God. Bonhoeffer was known for teachings, but also his methods for studying scripture. He had all students spend 30 minutes everyday with a single verse and meditate on that verse. Not relying on other commentaries for revelation, or for seeking the understanding provided by others. Instead, he believed that each of them had a Helper with them that can provide true revelation. They had regular readings (going through the psalms monthly, and morning and evening teachings), but they also had wonderful fun. Regularly Bonhoeffer would sit at the piano and the music would fill the air, or the soccer ball would come out, or maybe a ping-pong tournament. It was a true community, with a mission of seeking God.

Eventually, the Gestapo closed Finkenwalde. At that point, 113 students had been trained, but the students there at the shutdown were conscripted into the army of the Third Reich or sent to the concentration camps. It was at this time all Germans of age were being given the choice to join the army or face the consequences. This forced Bonhoeffer to decide what he was going to do next. He was banned from publicly preaching in Germany and was likely to be conscripted into the military at any moment. He decided to take an invitation form Union Theological Seminary in New York. Providing him a haven from which the Third Reich can't reach.

This lasted six weeks, upon which Bonhoeffer had convictions in his heart that he left his family, his friends, and his country, and knew God could use him in Germany. Being back in Germany Bonhoeffer met with his brother-in-law Hans Dohnanyi brought him into the Abwehr, the Third Reich intelligence, to protect him from the conscription.

It was here that Bonhoeffer functioned as a double agent for the conspiracy. His official title was counterintelligence officer for the Abwehr. His job was to gather intelligence for the German military about foreign influences, but secretly he used this as a cover to help the resistance. Bonhoeffer had the cover to travel internationally, and his goal was to inform England of the assassination plan in hopes that England would (1) help in planning and (2) ensure that once Hitler was dead England would end the war peacefully. To this end, Bonhoeffer met with his collegue Bishop Bell, who had close ties to the Anthony Eden, to try and pass information from the resistance to the British leadership. Unfortunately, the British were unwilling to be a part of an assassination attempt considering the possibility that it would lead to a complete invasion of England by Nazi forces if it failed. This was furthered by the Allied powers wanting an unconditional surrender of Nazi Germany.

While in this position as double agent, Bonhoeffer was able to help smuggle Jews out of the country into Switzerland. This plan would later be known as operation seven (though the plan expanded to saving 14 Jews).

He would continue this work in the Abwehr from 1940 until his arrest on April 5th 1943. They came to him and his parents' home, and Bonhoeffer walked out with his Bible in his hand and got into the black van [p. 433]. This would be the last time he would ever set foot in that home. From there, he was taken to a prison in Berlin, Tegel military prison, where he would send his letters that would later make up the book Letters and Papers from Prison.

This makes us beg the question, how is a pacifist theologian going to handle prison? The answer, strangely well. His first letter back to his parents he notes, "Strangely enough, the discomforts that one generally associates with prison life, the physical hardships, hardly bother me at all. A violent mental upheaval such as is produced by a sudden arrest brings with it the need to take one's mental bearings and come to terms with an entirely new situation all this means that physical things take a back seat and lose their importance, and it is something that I find to be a real enrichment of my experience. I am not so unused to being alone as other people are, and it is certainly a good spiritual Turkish bath. Besides that, I have my Bible and some reading matter from the library here, and enough writing paper now." [Letters p.22]. Admittedly some of this was likely to help comfort his parents. His first two weeks were in complete solitary confinement, but this, and the remainder of his imprisonment, is where we see into the Truth seeking that Bonhoeffer had for the Lord's Will.

"From the beginning of his time until the end, Bonhoeffer maintained the daily discipline of scriptural meditation and prayer he had been practicing for more than a decade. Each morning, he meditated for at least half an hour on a verse of Scripture. And he interceded for his friends and relatives, and for his brothers in the Confessing Church who were on the front lines or in concentration camps. Once he got his Bible back he read it for hours each day. By November [8 months after his arrest], he had read the Old Testament two and a half times. He also drew strength from praying the Psalms, just as they had done at Zingst, Finkenwalde, Schlawe, Sigurdshof, and elsewhere. Bonhoeffer once told Bethge, who was about to embark on a trip, that it was all the more important to practice the daily disciplines when away, to give oneself a sense of grounding and continuity and clarity" [p. 438]

We could go on and talk about the details of the various assassination attempts (of which there were many that Bonhoeffer was involved in or connected to). We could go on to talk about the fact he became the de facto pastor for the prison, where guards would come to him for advice. We could go on to give more details about how his writings have continued to be classics and insightful Christian literature. We could even note how his time spent in racially segregated America prepared him for the signs of racism in Germany. But what I think Bonhoeffer would rather have us do is simply look back at the Word of God. Bonhoeffer is interesting, I think, because of his unquestionable, unwavering, and unbreakable desire to follow the Will of God, even and especially when it goes into uncomfortable territory. Should a Christian go against the state? Should a Christian be willing to lie and spy and act as a double agent against the state? Should a Christian help plot an assassination? I don't think these questions have any good answers, but Bonhoeffer was so soaked in the Word of God, that I can't help but believe his answers couldn't have been far from God's Will.

Bonhoeffer would remain in prison until April 9, 1943, when he was hung after Hitler found the diaries of a fellow conspiracy member and ordered all members associated with the assassinations executed immediately.

At his death, the prison doctor noted, "I saw Pastor Bonhoeffer kneeling on the floor, praying fervently to God so certain that God heard his prayer. I have hardly ever seen a man die so entirely submissive to the will of God." [p. 237] "What made him stand out was that he did not hope that God heard his prayers, but knew it." [p. 237]

I want to be clear, a martyr is "a person who is killed because of their religious or other beliefs." Bonhoeffer was not arrested for being Christian. He was arrested in his family home in 1943 for his part in Operation 7 where 14 Jews were smuggled across the border to Switzerland. Nor was Bonhoeffer sentenced death for being Christian. He was sentenced to death by SS judge Otto Thorbeck for his part in the plot to kill Hitler. But through all of this it was a belief in God's plan for him that led him to take the actions he did, and because of this he certainly has died for of his beliefs. For if not for the convictions God placed in his heart, he might have lived through the four weeks that separated his death and Germany's surrender. He was killed for his actions against the Third Reich, but he died for his unyielding service to the cross. He truly was a citizen of Heaven.

I want to end this talk with a quote from Bonhoeffer himself, which I think encapsulates his life significantly better than I ever could. We have to challenge ourselves to consider how we in our own lives can fight the good fight [2 Tim. 4:7], persevere under trial [James 1:12], and hold fast [Phil. 2:16] to the confession of our hope without wavering [Heb 10:23].
